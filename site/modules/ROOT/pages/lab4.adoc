== From Filings to Searchable Knowledge

Before diving into the retrieval strategies, here is how the data in this lab was prepared and why each step matters.

. **The raw data is SEC 10-K filings.** These are annual financial reports that public companies file with the Securities and Exchange Commission. A single filing can run tens of thousands of words covering business operations, risk factors, financial results, and more.

. **Filings are split into chunks.** A full filing is too long to search precisely, so each one is split into smaller passages called chunks. Each chunk becomes a node in the knowledge graph, linked to its source document and to adjacent chunks so the original reading order is preserved.

. **Each chunk is encoded as an embedding.** An embedding model reads the chunk's text and produces a numerical vector that captures what the text _means_, not just what words it contains. "Supply chain disruption" and "logistics bottleneck risk" produce similar vectors because they describe related concepts, even though they share no keywords.

. **Vector search finds chunks by meaning.** Given a natural-language question, the system encodes it using the same embedding model and searches for stored chunk vectors whose meaning is closest. This is semantic similarity search: it matches on concepts rather than exact words.

. **Vector search returns isolated passages.** The results are ranked text fragments with no surrounding context. You get the chunk text and a similarity score, but nothing about which company filed it, what products it mentions, or what risks it describes.

. **Graph-enriched search follows relationships to fill that gap.** The knowledge graph connects chunks to documents, companies, products, and risk factors through typed relationships. After vector search finds relevant chunks, graph traversal follows those relationships to pull in structured context. The result is not just "here's a matching passage" but "here's a matching passage from Apple's 10-K, mentioning the iPhone, linked to a cybersecurity risk factor."

This lab covers steps 4 through 6. The chunking and embedding were pre-computed and are loaded in Notebook 01. xref:lab6.adoc[Lab 6] covers building that data pipeline from scratch.

== The Two-Layer Graph

The graph you explored in Lab 1 — companies, products, risk factors, asset managers — is the **structured layer**. Notebook 01 adds the **unstructured layer**: Document and Chunk nodes with embeddings, linked by `FROM_DOCUMENT` and `NEXT_CHUNK` relationships. Cross-links (`FROM_CHUNK`) connect entities like Products and RiskFactors to the Chunks that mention them.

The `FILED` relationship bridges the two layers: `(Company)-[:FILED]->(Document)`. When graph-enriched search traverses from a chunk to its source document to the company that filed it, it crosses from unstructured text into the structured entities you already know. That traversal chain — `(Chunk)-[:FROM_DOCUMENT]->(Document)<-[:FILED]-(Company)` — is what makes GraphRAG possible.

[source]
----
Structured Layer (Lab 1)          Unstructured Layer (Lab 4 Notebook 01)
┌──────────────────────┐          ┌──────────────────────────────────────┐
│ (:Company)           │──FILED──▶│ (:Document)◀─FROM_DOCUMENT─(:Chunk) │
│   ├─OFFERS─▶Product  │          │                 │                   │
│   ├─FACES_RISK─▶Risk │          │            NEXT_CHUNK               │
│   └─COMPETES_WITH    │          │                 ▼                   │
│ (:AssetManager)      │          │             (:Chunk)                │
│   └─OWNS─▶Company   │          └──────────────────────────────────────┘
└──────────────────────┘
         ▲                                    │
         └────── FROM_CHUNK ──────────────────┘
         (Products, RiskFactors linked to Chunks)
----

== Retrieval Strategies

=== VectorRetriever

Notebook 02 introduces the `VectorRetriever` from the neo4j-graphrag library. It wraps the vector index and handles embedding the query, running the similarity search, and formatting the results. Connected to an LLM via `GraphRAG`, it builds a complete question-answering pipeline. The results are chunk text ranked by cosine similarity — isolated passages with no graph context.

=== VectorCypherRetriever

Notebook 03 extends vector search with the `VectorCypherRetriever`. After the vector index finds matching chunks, a custom Cypher retrieval query runs on each match to traverse the graph and pull in structured context:

[source,cypher]
----
MATCH (node)-[:FROM_DOCUMENT]->(doc:Document)
OPTIONAL MATCH (doc)<-[:FILED]-(company:Company)
OPTIONAL MATCH (company)-[:FACES_RISK]->(risk:RiskFactor)
OPTIONAL MATCH (product:Product)-[:FROM_CHUNK]->(node)
----

The LLM now receives the matched chunk alongside the filing accession number, company name, product list, and risk factor labels — producing answers grounded in both unstructured text and structured knowledge graph relationships.
